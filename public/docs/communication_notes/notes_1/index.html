Amplitude modulation

background

m(t) be the message to be transmitted.

it carries information. Analog or digital.

assume it is baseband, whose spectrum is centered at 0.

baseband is only suitable for wired transmissions: the frequency.

for optical or ratio: shift the baseband to certain frequency ranges that is suitable for communications.

so the baseband -> passband, which is called modulation.

process

baseband signal m(t) -> modulation sys -> passband signal s(t) -> channel -> demodulation -> estimation of m(t). 调制解调。

tool

signal: carrier c(t): usually sinusoidal. c(t) = Ac*cos(2*%pi*fc*t+thetac)

how to embed info: amplitude modulation

idea

use amplitude of carrier signal to carry the message m(t)

  • $s(t) = A_c(1+K_am(t))cos(2\pi f_ct)$

    1. 1 + Ka*m(t): make it greater than zero -> abs(Ka*m(t)) < 1

      Ka -> amplitude sensitivity: to make sure there is no overlap in the passband signal s(t).

      if it is not satisfied, overmodulation.

    2. fc should be sufficiently high. like sampling. (>2*W), W is the bandwidth of m(t).

    mul the sinusoidal: create the envelope.

    Turn the frequency info to amplitude.

spectrum of AM signal

  • Dirac delta funciton

    complex -> delta(f - fc)

    cos(2%pifc) -> 1/2[delta(f-fc)+delta(f+fc)]

  • drawback:

    • power inefficient as transmitted sinusoidal does not contain any message information
    • bandwidth inefficient since the M(f) is even (symmetric)

  • demodulation

demodulation of AM signal: envelop detector

Remind that we have AM signal like: $$ s(t) = (1 + K_am(t))cos(2\pi f_ct) $$ The carrier signal is the base signal used in transmission, the $K_a$ is amplitude sensitivity to scale the message to amplitude range that $1+K_am(t)$ > 0.

The spectrum: $ \begin{flalign} S(f) &= F[\frac12(1+K_am(t))(e^{i2\pi f_ct}+e^{-i2\pi f_ct})]\
&= \frac12[\delta(f-f_c)+K_aM(f-f_c)+\delta(f+f_c)+K_aM(f+f_c)]\
\end{flalign} $

First the signal should go through the Half-wave Rectifier to cut negative half part of the signal. Then the signal will become: $$ v(t) = (1+K_am(t))w(t)\
\text {where } w(t) = \begin{cases} 0 & {-\frac{T_0}2\le t \le -\frac {T_0}4}\
A_ccos(2 \pi f_c t) & {-\frac {T_0}4 \le t \le \frac {T_0}4}\
0 & {\frac {T_0}4 \le t \le \frac {T_0}2} \end{cases} \text { And }T_0 = \frac 1f_c $$ since $w(t)$ is of period $\frac1{f_c}$ , we can express it as Fourier series: $$ w(t) = \sum {n=-\infty}^{\infty} c_ne^{j\frac {2\pi}{T_0}nt}\
\text {where } c_n = \frac 1{T_0}\int {T_0} w(t)e^{-j\frac {2\pi}{T_0}nt} dt $$ Then the Fourier transform of $v(t)$: $$ V(f) = F(v(t)) = F[(1+K_am(t))w(t)]\
= F((1+K_a)m(t)\cdot\sum{n=-\infty}^{\infty}c_ne^{j\frac {2\pi}{T_0}nt})\
= \sum{n=-\infty}^{\infty}c_nF((1+K_am(t))e^{j\frac{2\pi}{T_0}nt})\
= \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty}c_n F(e^{j\frac{2\pi}{T_0}nt}+K_am(t)e^{j\frac{2\pi}{T_0}nt})\
= \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty}c_n [\delta(f-\frac{n}{T_0})+K_aM(f-\frac{n}{T_0})] $$ Then feed the signal to the LPF, leaving only $n=0$ part: $$ \Rightarrow c_0[\delta(f)+K_aM(f)] $$ Apply inverse Fourier transform: $$ \Rightarrow c_0[1+K_am(t)] $$ Still, we have $c_0$ unknown. Then non-coherent receiver since the demodulation process is not continuous.

Recall that $$ c_n = \frac1{T_0}\int_{T_0}v(t)e^{-j\frac{2\pi}{T_0}nt}dt $$ Then $c_0$ is $$ c_0 = \frac1{T_0}\int_{T_0}v(t)dt\
= \frac1{T_0} \int_{-\frac{T_0}4} ^{\frac{T_0}4} A_ccos(2\pi f_ct)dt\
= \frac{A_c}{T_0} \cdot \frac{1}{2\pi f_c} \cdot sin(2\pi f_c t) \bigg\rvert_{-\frac{T_0}4} ^{\frac{T_0}4}\
= \frac{A_c}{T_0} \cdot \frac{1}{2\pi f_c} \cdot (sin(\frac\pi2)-sin(-\frac\pi2))\
= \frac{A_c}\pi $$

so the output of the envelop detector is $$ \frac{A_c}\pi (1+K_am(t)) $$ Bandwidth: $W \lt B \lt f_c - W$, in which $W$ is the bandwidth of the message signal. $(f_c > 2W)$

The drawbacks:

​ power inefficient: extra sinusoidal signal with no message: the constant term.

​ the scale factor may result in the loss of information for drastically changing signals.

​ bandwidth inefficient: negative part is duplicate: real -> symmetric & both side of the spectrum

Advantage:

​ Simple receiver

DSB-SC

So consider new modulation: $$ s(t) = m(t)A_ccos(2\pi f_ct)\
\text {where } c(t) = A_ccos(2\pi f_ct) \text { (carrier function)} $$

the spectrum:

peaks with scale $\frac{A_c}2$ on $-f_c$ and $f_c$.

the reason why scale and phase here is $cos(2\pi f_ct) = \frac12(e^{j2\pi f_ct}+e^{-j2\pi f_ct})$.

the spectrum is like: $$ F(s(t)) = \frac{A_c}2[M(f-f_c) + M(f+f_c)] $$

The coherent demodulation:

$$ s(t-t_0) \rightarrow \text {product modulation} \rightarrow \text {LPF} \rightarrow \hat m(t)\
\text {in product modulation } c’(t-t_0) \text { is fed} $$

The frequency and time of the carrier function in product modulation should sync with the input signal.

$A_c$: amplitude of carrier when modulating the signal. $$ \begin{flalign} c(t) &= A_c’cos(2\pi f_ct)\\
v(t) &= s(t)A_c’cos(2\pi f_ct)\
&= (m(t)A_ccos(2\pi f_ct))\cdot A_c’cos(2\pi f_ct)\
&= A_c\cdot A_c’\cdot m(t)cos^2(2\pi f_ct)\\
\end{flalign} $$ since $cos^2(a) = \frac{1+cos(2a)}2$, $$ \begin{flalign} RHS &= \frac{A_cA_c’}2 \cdot m(t)[1+cos(4\pi f_ct)]\
&= \frac{A_cA_c’}2 \cdot m(t) + \frac{A_cA_c’}2 \cdot cos(4\pi f_ct)\
&= \text {desired message} + \text {high frequency component} \end{flalign} $$ $v(t)$ as before, do Fourier transform to see the spectrum: $$ V(f) = A_c’\cdot F(s(t)cos(2\pi f_ct))\
= \frac {A_c’} 2 [S(f-f_c) + S(f+f_c)] \text { (tear cos() apart)}\
$$ in which $S(f) = \frac {A_c} 2 (M(f-f_c) + M(f+f_c))$

then the spectrum: $$ \frac {A_c’} 2 [\frac {A_c} 2 (M(f-2f_c) + M(f)) + \frac {A_c} 2 (M(f) + M(f+2f_c))]\
\Rightarrow \frac {A_cA_c’} 4 M(f-2f_c) + \frac {A_cA_c’} 2 M(f) + \frac {A_cA_c’} 4 M(f+2f_c)) $$ which is three peaks with scale $\frac {A_cA_c’} 2$ at 0, scale $\frac {A_cA_c’} 4$ at $2f_c$ and $-2f_c$ (spectrum)

LPF: just get the $\frac {A_cA_c’} 2M(f)$

Bandwidth: $W\le BW \le f_c$

synchronization error

e.g. $$ c’(t) = A_c’cos(2\pi f_c(t-t_0)) \
= A_c’cos(2\pi f_ct+\theta) \
$$ where $\theta=2\pi f_ct_0$.

then $v(t)$ becomes $$ v(t) = [m(t)A_ccos(2\pi f_ct)]A_c’cos(2\pi f_ct+\theta) \
= A_cA_c’m(t)cos(2\pi f_ct)cos(2\pi f_ct+\theta) \
$$ since $cos(a)cos(b) = \frac12 cos(a-b)+\frac12 cos(a+b)$ $$ v(t) = \frac {A_cA_c’}2m(t)cos(\theta)+\frac {A_cA_c’}2m(t)cos(4\pi f_ct + \theta) $$ then apply LPF, purge the high freq part, the m(t) will be:

$m(t) = \frac {A_cA_c’}2 m(t) cos(\theta)$

SSB-SC (single sideband)

  • after DSB_SC the peaks after f~c~ in spectrum is upper sideband, while the other is lower sideband. (the abs value determines the upper & lower, for the negative side)

    The spectrum is: $$ S(f) = \frac{A_c}2[M(f-f_c) + M(f+f_c)] $$ After product modulation the signal goes through the ‘BPF’, only let the USB to pass.

    BPF means ‘band pass filter’, which only allows certain frequencies to pass.

    The result will be: $$ S_{SSB-SC}(f) = \frac{A_c}2[M_+(f-f_c) + M_-(f+f_c)] $$ Note that the $M_-$ is just negate of $M_+$, not the other side of spectrum of $f_c$.

  • is that possible to recover the m(t)?

    similar to that in DSB_SC. The core idea is to reduce the frequency shifting by multiplying sinusoidal.

    The $V(f)$ after product modulation: $$ \begin{flalign} V(f) &= \frac{A_c’}2[S_{SSB}(f-f_c)+S_{SSB}(f+f_c)]\
    &= \frac{A_c’}2[\frac{A_c}2[M_+(f-2f_c) + M_-(f)] + \frac{A_c}2[M_+(f) + M_-(f+2f_c)]]\
    &= \frac{A_cA_c’}4M_+(f-2f_c)+\frac{A_cA_c’}2(M_+(f)+M_-(f))+\frac{A_cA_c’}4M_-(f+2f_c)\
    \end{flalign} $$ Since $M_+(f)+M_-(f) = M(f)$ $$ \begin{flalign} RHS = \frac{A_cA_c’}4M_+(f-2f_c) + \frac{A_cA_c’}4M(f) + \frac{A_cA_c’}4M_-(f+2f_c)\
    \end{flalign} $$ Then feed to LPF: $$ \hat M(f) = \frac{A_cA_c’}4M(f)\
    \hat m(t) = \frac {A_cA_c’}4m(t) $$

QAM

Transmission of two messages

two message simultaneously:

two signal with different carrier: with shift $\frac {\pi}2$ . $$ c_1(t) = A_ccos(2\pi f_ct)\
c_2(t) = A_csin(2\pi f_ct) $$ then $$ S_{GAN} = c_1 + c_2 $$ Quadrature Amplitude Modulation QAM

Assume DSBSC first: $$ m_1(t) \rightarrow \text {mul mod } \rightarrow \
m_2(t) \rightarrow \text {mul mod with 90 phase shift} \rightarrow $$ Thus the carrier for $m_1$ and $m_2$ are: $$ c_1(t) = A_ccos(2\pi f_ct)\
c_2(t) = A_csin(2\pi f_ct) $$ Then $$ \begin{flalign} S_{QAM}(t) &= m_1(t)A_ccos(2\pi f_ct)+m_2(t)A_csin(2\pi f_ct)\
S_{QAM}(f) &= F(S_{QAM}(t))\
&= \frac{A_c}2[M_1(f-f_c)+M_1(f+f_c)]+\frac{A_c}{2j}[M_2(f-f_c)-M_2(f+f_c)]\
&= S_1(f) + S_2(f)\
\end{flalign} $$ Where $-j = \frac1j$

Then draw the $S_1(f)$ and $(-j)S_2(f)$:

Angle modulation

General expression of passband signal $$ s(t) = a(t)cos(2\pi f_ct+\phi(t)) $$ In angle, the phase carries the messages instead of amplitude. The frequency will be changing.

Here the average power is a constant: $A_ccos()\rightarrow \frac{A_c^2}2$

Good because: power amplifier: linear in certain input range. If the amplitude is fixed, it will be easier to determine the component. ($m(t)\rightarrow\alpha m(t)$)

angle modulated signal

$$ s(t) = A_ccos(\theta_{i}(t))\
$$

where $\theta_i(t) \in [0, 2\pi]$

Average frequency: $\frac{\theta_i(t+\Delta t)-\theta_i(t)}{2\pi \Delta t}$

Instantaneous frequency: limit to 0. $f_i(t) = \frac1{2\pi}\theta_i’(t)$

$\theta_i(t) - \theta_i(0) = \int _0 ^t f_i(\tau)d\tau$

1. Phase modulation (PM)

suppose the message is $m(t)$, let $\theta_i(t) = 2\pi f_ct+k_pm(t)$, where $k_p$ is called phase sensitivity.

then $$ S_{PM}(t) = A_ccos(2\pi f_ct+k_pm(t)) $$ the instantaneous freq: $f_i(t) = \frac {1}{2\pi} (2\pi f_c+k_p\frac {dm(t)}{dt}) = f_c + \frac{k_p}{2\pi} \frac{dm(t)}{dt}$

2. Frequency modulation (FM)

Directly let the message to be embedded in instantaneous freq $$ f_i(t) = f_c + k_fm(t) $$ , where $f_c$ is a frequency carrier.

then $$ \begin{flalign} \theta_i(t) &= 2\pi \cdot \int _0 ^t f_c + k_fm(t)dt\
&= 2\pi f_c t + 2\pi k_f\int 0 ^tm(t)dt\
\end{flalign} $$ Thus $$ S{FM} (t) = A_ccos(2\pi f_ct + 2\pi k_f\int _0 ^t m(t)dt) $$

Relation between PM and FM

$$ PM(m(t)) = FM(\frac1{2\pi} \frac{k_p}{k_f} \frac{dm(t)}{dt})\
FM(m(t)) = PM(2\pi \frac{k_f} {k_p} \int _0 ^t m(t)dt)\
$$

Because of the relation we only focus on FM since all of its properties can be easily mapped to PM

in which $e^{j\beta sin(2\pi f_mt)}$ is conjugate symmetric: $f(t) = f^*(-t)$

Then the Fourier transform is real value.

Properties of Bessel function:

  • $J_n(\beta) = (-1)^nJ_{-n}(\beta)$

  • If $\beta$ is small, $n=0$, $J_0(\beta) \approx 1$

  • If $\beta$ is small, $n=1$, $J_1(\beta)\approx \beta$

  • If $\beta$ is small, $n > 1$, $J_n(\beta) \approx 0$

Then the spectrum has only three left: $n = 0, 1, 2$, which is consistent to the Narrowband case.

$\beta$ is large, theoretically the spectrum is infinite.

For large $\beta$, $B_i\approx 2\Delta f$

Combine them two: $$ B_i=2\Delta f(1+\frac1\beta)\
B_i=2\Delta f+ 2f_m\
